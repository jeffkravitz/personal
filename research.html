Research

My graduate research has focused on topics including moral judgment, emotion, stress, mindfulness, and decision making. These projects have drawn upon social, cognitive, and evolutionary psychology in order to produce impactful, multidisciplinary research. More recently, my work at New York University utilized neuroimaging methods including functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) and Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) to investigate the neural mechanisms that support visual cognition and visual working memory. One of my biggest projects for the Curtis Lab was developing a new method to stimulate the visual field for population receptive field (pRF) modeling. pRF modeling involves systematically stimulating different parts of your vision while being scanned by an MRI machine, and then using the resulting data and structure to determine which parts of your visual cortex encode which regions of space. The goal of the new method was to attain more robust results for downstream brain regions while making the method more accessible by using well-documented, open source python code. I designed and coded this new method in Python, programming MRI compatibility and a number of supporting functionality including environment setup and installation scripts, data structure reports based on runtime parameters, and stimuli image processing. I collected pRF data using the university's MRI machine, and supported our sister lab at Columbia University when they adopted our procedure to examine the visual cortices of schizophreniia patients.

While working as the lab manageer for the Curtis Lab, I was trained and certified in Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) operation. This is an experimental technique that we use to stimulate a region of the brain during a visual working memory task to determine whether that area of the brain plays a role in the completion of the task. This involves using 3D neuronavigation software to target the correct region of interest, set up and position the magnetic coil properly, launch the experiment, and observe and manage the position of the coil to ensure that subject movement does not displace the coil, resulting in stimulation of an incorrect brain region. Additionaly, I set up Python compatibility with the TMS system for our lab, and collected data for various experiments to support the work of several doctoral students.

Aphantasia, or the condition in which a person cannot create visual images in their mind, has recently become a topic of interest for neuroscientists. This inablity to mentally visualize can offer much insight into the way that visual cognition works. In order to characterize the cognitive functioning associated with this deficit, one of my projects involved developing and coordinating a multistage online psychophysics study. In this study, I recruited subjects from online communities and directed them through a series of three experiments created with Python's PsychoPy and hosted on Pavlovia. These experiments included a mental rotation task, a change detection task, and a pattern matching task. I then created a data analysis pipeline to process and analyze the resulting data, creating a cognitive profile for the subjects.

My thesis research at Brooklyn College focused on the distinct social functions of disgust and anger in morality. Specifically, I theorized that anger, an approach-tendency emotion, causes us to engage with those who have transgressed to elicit behavioral changes in the transgressor, placing us in immediate precarious situations but maximizing positive outcomes in the future. In contrast, when a transgressor has stable, negative traits (bad moral character) the likelihood of inciting behavioral change decreases, and the avoidance-tendencies of disgust instead function to withdraw from the transgressor, reducing the likelihood of becoming the target of future immoral behavior.

In a second line of research, I developed a study that aims to determine whether automatic processes such as stress have a causal role in moral judgment or if they are merely the result of such judgment. This study utilizes false physiological feedback to test the hypothesis that perceived stress causes more severe moral judgment. In the experiment, participants wear headphones and are told that they are listening to their own heartbeat; in reality, they are randomly assigned to hear either a fast or normal heartbeat. I hypothesized that participants in the fast-heartbeat condition will attribute their perceived stress response to moral transgressions presented in a vignette, and thus judge them more severely. Further, I hypothesized that this effect will be moderated by mindfulness such that people high in mindfulness will be aware of and non-reactive to their physiological feedback, and thus judge less severely than those lower in mindfulness.

In Stony Brook University’s Psychology Honors Program, I conducted independent research under the guidance of my mentor and research advisor Dr. Nancy Franklin. My honors project focused on whether mindfulness meditation can reduce inherent bias in decision-making. People have the tendeny to devalue future rewards, a phenomenon known as delay discounting. Delay discounting is widely used as a measure of impulsivity. Mindfulness meditation is a practice that emphasizes a present-focused, non-reactive awareness of one’s experiences; in essence, control over one’s attention and emotions. I hypothesized that a single session of guided mindfulness meditation would reduce delay discounting rates by causing people to be non-reactive to their impulses. The results of the study supported this idea.

As my introduction to psychology research as an undergraduate, I worked in Dr. Nancy Franklin’s memory lab. In the lab, I worked on a project that aimed to determine the effect of anger on memory through the use of a boundary extension paradigm. The primary hypothesis of this study was that anger, an approach-tendency emotion, causes people to focus on central aspects of experience, and would thus cause recognition of a zoomed-in version of an original image.
